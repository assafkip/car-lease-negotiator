# Chris Voss Negotiation Playbook for Car Deals Over Email

Based on "Never Split the Difference" and Voss's public teachings (MasterClass, NPR Planet Money, Black Swan Group).

---

## 1. CORE TECHNIQUES WITH EXAMPLES

### 1A. Tactical Empathy & Labeling

**What it is:** Understanding the other party's emotions and perspective, then naming those emotions out loud to defuse them.

**Phrases:**
- "It seems like you're under a lot of pressure to move inventory this month..."
- "It sounds like you've already given this your best consideration..."
- "It looks like there's not a lot of flexibility on your end..."

**Why it works:** When you label someone's emotion accurately, it reduces the intensity of that emotion. The brain's amygdala calms down. The person feels heard and becomes more collaborative.

**Key rule:** Never say "I understand" -- that's about YOU. Labels are about THEM.

---

### 1B. Mirroring (Repeating Last 1-3 Words)

**What it is:** Repeating the last 1-3 critical words the other person said, followed by silence.

**Example:**
- Dealer: "This is our absolute best price."
- You: "Your absolute best price?"
- (Silence. They fill it with more information.)

**Why it works:** Triggers the other person to elaborate, clarify, or reveal more information. People feel compelled to explain when their words are reflected back.

---

### 1C. Calibrated Questions

**What it is:** Open-ended "How" and "What" questions that put the problem on the other party. Never use "Why" (feels accusatory).

**Key phrases:**
- "How am I supposed to do that?" (Voss's favorite -- used in the actual car purchase)
- "What does a good outcome look like for you?"
- "How does this pricing compare to what other customers are getting?"
- "What happens if I need to walk away from this deal?"
- "How can I justify this number to my spouse?"

**Why it works:** Forces the other side to solve YOUR problem. Gives them the illusion of control while you steer the conversation.

---

### 1D. The Accusation Audit

**What it is:** Listing every negative thing the other party might think about you BEFORE they say it. Sounds exaggerated when spoken aloud, which makes them want to correct you.

**Example opening:**
"You're probably thinking I'm just another buyer trying to lowball you. You might feel like I'm wasting your time. You may even think I'm not serious about buying..."

**Why it works:** By voicing the worst-case accusations yourself, you defuse them. The other party almost always responds with "No, no, that's not what I think at all" and becomes more open.

---

### 1E. "No"-Oriented Questions

**What it is:** Framing questions so the answer you want is "No" instead of "Yes." People feel safer saying no -- it preserves their sense of autonomy and control.

**Phrases:**
- "Would it be ridiculous to consider $X?"
- "Is it a bad idea to ask for the money factor?"
- "Would it be unreasonable to see a full fee breakdown?"
- "Have you given up on working with me on this?"
- "Is it a terrible idea to get competing quotes?"

**Why it works:** "Yes" feels like commitment and makes people defensive. "No" feels safe. When someone says "No, it's not ridiculous," they've just agreed to your frame.

---

### 1F. The Late-Night FM DJ Voice

**What it is:** A calm, slow, downward-inflecting voice that conveys confidence and control. Think late-night radio host.

**Rules:**
- Slow down
- Lower your pitch slightly
- Use a downward inflection (declarative, not questioning)
- Calm is contagious

**Email equivalent:** Short sentences. Periods instead of exclamation marks. No urgency words. Measured, confident tone.

---

### 1G. The "That's Right" Moment

**What it is:** Getting your counterpart to say "That's right" (not "You're right"). This means they genuinely feel understood.

**How to trigger it:** Summarize their position so accurately that they have no choice but to say "That's right."

**Example:** "So it sounds like you've been trying to give me the best number you can, and you're limited by what the dealership will approve, and you want to get this deal done as much as I do. Is that fair?"

**"You're right" = they want you to shut up. "That's right" = breakthrough.**

---

### 1H. Black Swans (Hidden Game-Changers)

**What it is:** Unknown unknowns -- hidden information that, once discovered, changes the entire negotiation dynamic. Voss says there are at least 3 in every negotiation.

**Car deal Black Swans might include:**
- Dealer has a monthly/quarterly sales target and needs 2 more deals by Friday
- The specific car has been on the lot 60+ days (carrying cost pressure)
- A newer model year is about to hit and they need to clear inventory
- The salesperson is close to a bonus tier
- The dealership has a wholesale buyer lined up if retail fails
- Factory incentives that aren't publicly advertised

**How to find them:** Calibrated questions, mirroring, and patient silence. They leak out when the other person feels comfortable talking.

---

### 1I. Ackerman Bargaining Model (65-85-95-100%)

**The system:**
1. Set your TARGET price (what you actually want to pay)
2. First offer: 65% of target
3. Second offer: 85% of target (raise of 20%)
4. Third offer: 95% of target (raise of 10%)
5. Final offer: 100% of target (raise of 5%)

**Rules:**
- Each raise comes AFTER the other side makes a counter
- Use calibrated questions between each raise to slow things down
- Decreasing increments signal you're being squeezed dry
- Final number should be PRECISE and non-round (e.g., $34,287 not $34,000)
- On the final offer, throw in a non-monetary item they probably don't want (shows you're at your limit)

**Car example (target = $35,000):**
1. First offer: $22,750
2. After their counter: $29,750
3. After their counter: $33,250
4. Final: $35,087 + "and I'd need the first oil change included"

---

### 1J. Bending Reality with Deadlines & Loss Aversion

**Deadlines:** Most dealer deadlines are artificial. "This price is only good today" is almost never true. Voss's rule: deadlines are the boogeyman of negotiation. Resist the pressure.

**Loss aversion:** People fear losing something roughly 2x more than they value gaining the same thing. Frame your offers in terms of what the dealer LOSES by not accepting.

**Example:** "I'm ready to close today, but I do have an appointment at [other dealer] tomorrow. I'd hate for both of us to lose out on what seems like a great fit."

## 2. CAR-SPECIFIC APPLICATION OF EACH TECHNIQUE

### Labeling When Dealer Says "That Price Isn't Possible"

> "It sounds like there's real pressure from management on these numbers. It seems like you're working within some tight constraints. I respect that. What would it take to bridge the gap?"

You're acknowledging their reality without caving. This disarms the reflexive "no" and opens a door.

### Calibrated Questions When They Try to Get You to Visit

Dealer: "Why don't you come in so we can work something out?"

> "I appreciate that, and I definitely want to make this easy for both of us. How would a visit help us get closer on the numbers we haven't been able to agree on yet?"

Or more directly:

> "What specifically changes about the deal if I come in person?"

This forces them to reveal WHY they want you there (answer: they have more psychological leverage in person).

### Mirroring Over Email

Dealer email: "We've already discounted this vehicle significantly below MSRP."

Your reply: "Significantly below MSRP?"

That's it. One line. Wait for them to fill the silence with specifics.

### Accusation Audit in the First Email

> "I know I'm probably coming across as just another price shopper who's going to waste your time. You're probably thinking I'll just take your number and use it against another dealer. I completely get why you'd feel that way."

Then follow with your actual ask. They'll lean in because you disarmed the defensiveness before it could form.

### "No"-Oriented Questions for Better Pricing

- "Would it be ridiculous to ask what dealer cost is on this vehicle?"
- "Is it a terrible idea to ask for a breakdown of all the fees?"
- "Would you be opposed to matching what [Dealer X] offered me?"

### Detecting & Countering Dealer Manipulation

| Dealer Tactic | What They're Doing | Voss Counter |
|---|---|---|
| "What monthly payment are you looking for?" | Shifting to payment instead of price | "How does focusing on the total out-the-door price work for you?" |
| "Let me check with my manager" | Creating an invisible authority | "It seems like you've got some constraints. What does your manager typically look for in a deal they approve?" |
| "This offer expires today" | Artificial deadline pressure | "I understand there are time pressures. Is it a bad idea to take the night to make sure this is the right decision?" |
| Adding doc fees, dealer prep, etc. | Padding after the "deal" | "Help me understand -- what does this $895 dealer prep fee cover specifically?" |
| Refusing email pricing | Forcing in-person leverage | "It sounds like you prefer face-to-face. Would it be unreasonable to get a ballpark OTD number first so I know it's worth both our time?" |

### Ackerman Model Mapped to Car Price

**Scenario:** Car listed at $42,000. Your research says fair price is $37,000. Your TARGET is $36,500.

| Round | Your Offer | % of Target | Tactic |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $23,725 | 65% | Extreme anchor. Use accusation audit: "This might seem insulting, and I don't mean it to be..." |
| 2 | $31,025 | 85% | After their counter. Use calibrated Q: "How am I supposed to bridge this gap?" |
| 3 | $34,675 | 95% | After their counter. Label: "It seems like we're getting close..." |
| 4 | $36,487 | ~100% | Precise number + non-monetary: "...and I'd need the all-weather mats included." |

**Note on car deals:** The 65% anchor may be too extreme for cars where invoice pricing is public. Adjust -- you might start at 80% of target and use smaller increments. The PRINCIPLE matters more than the exact percentages: decreasing concessions that signal you're reaching your limit.

## 3. EMAIL-SPECIFIC ADAPTATIONS

### What You Lose Over Email
- **Tone of voice** -- The late-night DJ voice doesn't translate
- **Real-time mirroring** -- Less natural when there's a delay
- **Reading micro-expressions** -- Can't see their reaction
- **Pacing** -- Can't control conversational rhythm the same way

### What Works BETTER Over Email

**Calibrated questions:** Actually stronger in writing. The dealer can't dodge them as easily -- they're staring at the question on screen. Hard to ignore "How am I supposed to do that?" when it's in writing.

**Labeling:** Works well. Written labels feel considered and respectful, not manipulative. "It sounds like..." reads as thoughtful.

**Accusation audit:** Very effective. In writing, it reads as emotionally intelligent and disarming. The dealer has time to absorb it.

**"No"-oriented questions:** Excellent over email. "Would it be ridiculous to..." is powerful in writing because the reader has to sit with it.

**Precise numbers:** Even more impactful in writing. $34,287 looks like it came from careful calculation.

### What Doesn't Translate Well

**Mirroring:** Awkward in email if overused. One-word replies feel weird in writing. Use it sparingly -- once per email chain, max.

**DJ voice:** Impossible. Substitute with: short sentences, no exclamation marks, no ALL CAPS, no urgency language. Calm formatting = calm voice.

**Dynamic silence:** You can't be silent mid-conversation. But you CAN delay replies strategically (see below).

### Creating Tactical Silence Over Email

- **Delay responses by 4-24 hours.** Not ghosting -- just not being instantly available. Signals you have options and aren't desperate.
- **Send short replies.** "That's an interesting number." Then nothing else. Let them follow up.
- **One move per email.** Don't lay out your whole argument. Chess by mail. One move, then wait.
- **Ask a calibrated question and wait.** Don't follow up. Let the question do the work.

Voss calls this "delaying to save time." The slower you go, the more the other side reveals.

## 4. VOSS-STYLE LANGUAGE FOR SPECIFIC CAR DEALER SCENARIOS

### Scenario: Dealer Quotes Way Over Budget

> "I really appreciate you putting this together. It seems like there might be some room between where we are, and I want to be respectful of your time. How does $[your number] work as a starting point for the out-the-door price?"

If they reject:

> "It sounds like that number is tough to work with. What would need to happen to get closer to that range?"

### Scenario: Dealer Says "Come In and We'll Talk"

> "I'd love to get there, and I want to make sure it's a productive visit for both of us. Would it be unreasonable to nail down a ballpark OTD number first? That way neither of us wastes time."

If they push harder:

> "It seems like there's something that works better in person. What specifically changes about the deal if I come in?"

### Scenario: Dealer Adds Junk Fees

> "I want to make sure I understand the full picture. It looks like there are some line items here I wasn't expecting -- the $495 dealer prep and the $299 documentation fee. Help me understand what those cover specifically?"

Then:

> "Would it be unreasonable to look at the OTD price without those items? I'm comparing apples to apples across a few dealers."

### Scenario: Dealer Says "This Is Our Best Price"

> "I hear you, and it sounds like you've really gone to bat for me on this. I respect that. Before I make a decision, is there any flexibility on the fees side -- even something small -- that might help close the gap?"

If truly stuck:

> "It seems like we're close but not quite there. What would it take to get to $[your target]? I'm ready to sign today if we can find a way."

### Scenario: Dealer Tries to Rush You ("Offer Expires Today")

> "I appreciate the urgency, and I understand deals like this move fast. Is it a bad idea to take the evening to run the numbers one more time? I want to make sure I'm saying yes for the right reasons, not because I felt rushed."

**Do not acknowledge the deadline as real.** Voss's core lesson: deadlines are almost always flexible or fake.

### Scenario: Dealer Won't Give Numbers Over Email

> "I totally get that this is easier to discuss in person, and I don't want to put you in an awkward position. Would it be a terrible idea to just share a rough range so I know whether we're in the same ballpark? I've got a few options I'm weighing and I want to make sure you stay in the running."

The "stay in the running" framing triggers loss aversion -- they might lose you.

### Scenario: Using Competing Quotes Without Being Aggressive

> "I've been doing my homework, and I've gotten a few quotes in the $[X] range for a similar build. I'm not trying to play dealers against each other -- I genuinely like your dealership and would prefer to work with you. Is there a way to get close to that number?"

**Never bluff.** Only reference real quotes. And never CC dealers on the same email (despite what some guides say) -- it feels hostile.

### Scenario: Asking for Money Factor Transparency

> "I'm trying to understand the full cost of the lease. Would it be unreasonable to share the money factor you're using? I want to make sure I'm comparing this correctly to other offers."

If they dodge:

> "It seems like there might be some sensitivity around sharing that number. Help me understand what's behind the hesitation?"

### Scenario: Responding to "What Payment Are You Looking For?"

This is a trap. They'll stretch the loan term to hit your number while keeping the price high.

> "I appreciate the question. I'm actually more focused on the out-the-door price than the monthly payment. How does $[your target OTD] look on your end?"

If they push back on total price:

> "It sounds like the monthly payment approach works better for your process. What does the OTD price look like if we work backward from the payment you're suggesting?"

## QUICK REFERENCE CHEAT SHEET

| Technique | Email Phrase | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Label | "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." | Any time they push back or show emotion |
| Mirror | Repeat their last key phrase + "?" | When they make a claim you want them to elaborate on |
| Calibrated Q | "How am I supposed to..." / "What does that look like?" | When they make a demand or try to close |
| Accusation Audit | "You're probably thinking I'm just..." | First email or when re-engaging |
| No-Oriented | "Would it be ridiculous to..." | When making an ask that might feel bold |
| Tactical Silence | Wait 4-24 hours before replying | After making an offer or asking a calibrated Q |
| Ackerman | Precise numbers, decreasing increments | Price negotiation rounds |
| Loss Aversion | "I'd hate to lose out on..." | When you have competing options |
| "That's Right" | Summarize their position accurately | When you want to build rapport before an ask |
